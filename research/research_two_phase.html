Two-Phase Flows

Two-phase flow modelling and simulation currently represent my main research axis, which originated with my PhD thesis [Orl23]. My contributions address both the mathematical formulation of novel models and the development of suitable numerical methods.

During my PhD, I focused on incorporating geometric quantities that play a key role in interfacial dynamics, such as:

  • the interfacial area density, whose accurate evaluation is a prerequisite for reliable phase exchange terms [OBB23], and
  • the mean curvature of the interface, investigated from both modelling and numerical perspectives [OBB25].
In addition, I developed an extension of the numerical method originally designed for incompressible single-phase flows to two-phase flow configurations [Orl24].

More recently, I have been working on the analysis of a hierarchy of Baer-Nunziato-type models for compressible two-phase flows [Orl+25]. The objective of this work is to clarify the mathematical and numerical implications arising from the use of models that are not well posed in the classical sense, in particular due to the non-uniqueness of jump conditions associated with non-conservative products.

In parallel, a significant part of my current research is devoted to the derivation of two-phase flow models based on variational principles. While this framework was originally proposed for single-phase flows, with applications to the Euler-Fourier system [Hae+25a], it finds its most powerful application in the analysis of two-phase flows [Hae+25b], [Hae+26]. In particular, in [Hae+26], a novel multi-fluid model for compressible two-phase flows is proposed. The model is fully closed and introduces a new interfacial quantity,referred to as the interfacial work. The closures for the interfacial quantities are derived directly from the variational principle, ensuring physical consistency and well-defined behaviour across all flow topologies. The resulting system is shown to be hyperbolic, symmetrizable, and to admit an entropy conservation law. Moreover, its non-conservative products lead to uniquely defined jump conditions. A web page presenting relevant test cases can be found at https://hpc-maths.github.io/2025_09_two_fluid_all_topology/.

Finally, I am currently working on the development of unified multiscale models with mass transfer for two-phase flows. This approach aims at providing a unified and mathematically sound framework ensuring thermodynamic consistency, well-defined energy balances, and a clear interpretation of the inter-scale transfer terms (see animation).
